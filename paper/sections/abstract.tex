Large language models (LLMs) can propose improved versions of software artifacts ---
prompts, code, and configuration --- but existing LLM-guided optimization systems
lack the statistical rigor required for reliable evaluation and the structured
search mechanisms needed for systematic exploration of the artifact space.
Ad hoc mutation loops produce results that are difficult to reproduce,
compare, and trust.

We present \textsc{anneal}, a framework for statistically rigorous autonomous
artifact optimization. \textsc{anneal} organizes \NUM{16} enhancements across four
tracks: \emph{Representation} (structured manifests, component evolution),
\emph{Search} (dual-agent mutation, two-phase refinement, island populations),
\emph{Knowledge} (lineage context, episodic memory, research operators), and
\emph{Evaluation} (Wilcoxon signed-rank testing, Holm-Bonferroni correction,
adaptive sampling, Bradley-Terry scoring).

We evaluate \textsc{anneal} on a benchmark suite of \NUM{5} targets across three
domains (prompt optimization, code optimization, documentation quality) using
four configurations and ten independent runs per configuration.
\textsc{anneal} with all enhancements achieves statistically significant
improvement over the baseline on \NUM{X}/5 targets
($p < 0.05$ after Holm-Bonferroni correction, Cohen's $d = \NUM{X.X}$)
while reducing cost by \NUM{X}\% relative to the greedy baseline.
