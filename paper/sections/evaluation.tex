\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

We evaluate \textsc{anneal} on a benchmark suite designed to assess performance
across the artifact types the system targets.
The suite consists of five benchmark targets across three domains,
evaluated under four configurations with ten independent runs each.

% --- 5.2 Experimental Configurations ---
\subsection{Experimental Configurations}
\label{sec:eval-configs}

We use four configurations that form an ablation ladder:

\begin{description}
  \item[Baseline-Raw] Raw LLM generation with no optimization loop.
    We generate $N = 50$ artifact variants directly and report the best.
    This establishes a floor: what can LLMs do without search?

  \item[Baseline-Greedy] \textsc{anneal} with greedy hillclimbing only.
    No enhancement tracks are active. This isolates the contribution of
    the optimization loop itself from the enhancements.

  \item[Control] \textsc{anneal} version 0.2.0, pre-enhancement.
    All features available in the released codebase, with a monolithic
    \texttt{program.md} context.

  \item[Treatment] \textsc{anneal} with all \NUM{16} enhancements active
    (\cref{sec:enhancements}): Strategy Manifest, dual-agent mutation,
    two-phase mutation, lineage context, episodic memory, adaptive sampling,
    and Holm-Bonferroni correction.
\end{description}

% --- 5.3 Statistical Methodology ---
\subsection{Statistical Methodology}
\label{sec:eval-stats}

\textbf{Runs.}
We execute 10 independent runs per target-configuration pair, each using a
distinct random seed.
Independence is ensured by git worktree isolation: each run operates in a
separate worktree with no shared mutable state.
The total experiment count is
$5 \text{ targets} \times 4 \text{ configs} \times 10 \text{ runs} \times 50 \text{ experiments} = 10{,}000$ experiments.

\textbf{Significance test.}
To compare configurations on a given target, we apply the Wilcoxon signed-rank
test~\cite{wilcoxon1945} over the 10 paired final scores
(Control run $i$ vs. Treatment run $i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, 10$).
We use the signed-rank test rather than a $t$-test because the normality
assumption is unlikely to hold for bounded quality scores.

\textbf{Multiple comparison correction.}
We perform one comparison per target (Control vs. Treatment), yielding five
$p$-values.
We apply Holm-Bonferroni correction~\cite{holm1979} to control the
family-wise error rate at $\alpha = 0.05$.

\textbf{Effect size.}
We report Cohen's $d$ with 95\% bootstrap confidence intervals (2000 bootstrap
samples) to quantify the practical significance of observed improvements.

\textbf{Cost normalization.}
For cost efficiency analysis, we compare configurations at equal USD budget
rather than equal experiment count, accounting for the fact that Treatment
incurs higher per-experiment cost due to dual-agent mutation but may reach
target quality faster.

% --- 5.4 Metrics ---
\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:eval-metrics}

We collect the following metrics per run:

\begin{description}
  \item[$\text{score}_{50}$] Final score at experiment 50 (primary metric).
  \item[$\text{score}_{@\$X}$] Score at a fixed USD budget $X$, enabling
    cost-normalized comparison.
  \item[$\text{conv}_{90}$] Number of experiments to reach 90\% of the
    run's best score (convergence speed).
  \item[$\text{cost}_{\text{total}}$] Total API spend in USD for the 50-experiment run.
  \item[$\text{accept\_rate}$] Fraction of proposed mutations accepted
    (mutation acceptance rate).
  \item[$\text{cache\_hit}$] Evaluation cache hit rate (efficiency metric).
\end{description}
